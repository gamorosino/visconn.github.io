VISCONN

VISion CONNectivity toolbox

Repository Preprint Citation

Overview

VISCONN is a lightweight, open‑source toolbox for studying retinotopically informed connectivity within the human visual system. The toolbox ties retinotopic maps from functional MRI to structural connections derived from diffusion MRI, enabling analyses that respect the geometry of the visual field. VISCONN was developed as part of the project described in the preprint “A retinotopic wiring principle of the human brain,” which shows that regions representing the same location in visual space preferentially connect to one another.

Features

  • Retinotopic ROI generation: segment V1 and V2 into regions of interest based on polar angle and eccentricity.
  • Connectivity analysis: compare tractography between ROIs to reveal biases and asymmetries in early visual cortex.
  • Scalable workflow: process hundreds to thousands of participants using reproducible code and standard data formats.
  • Visualization utilities: plot maps and connectivity patterns to aid interpretation.

Installation

Clone the VISCONN repository and install in editable mode: 

git clone https://github.com/gamorosino/visconn.git
cd visconn
pip install -e .

VISCONN depends on standard neuroimaging libraries such as nibabel and numpy; see the repository for full requirements.

Study summary

The VISCONN framework was developed to map structural connectivity in the human visual system in retinotopic coordinates. By combining diffusion MRI tractography with retinotopic maps, VISCONN estimates connectivity between cortical locations defined by eccentricity and polar angle【854870623446578†L242-L269】. Using data from more than 1,700 participants across multiple datasets, the authors showed that connectivity between early visual areas follows a retinotopic “like-to-like” wiring principle: connections are strongest between homologous visual-field positions and decline monotonically with increasing distance between eccentricity bins【854870623446578†L284-L303】. This organization was robust across datasets and persisted after controlling for inter-regional distance and tractography biases【854870623446578†L304-L317】.

The study also linked structural connectivity to perceptual asymmetries. Connectivity between the lower vertical meridian (LVM), horizontal meridian (HM) and upper vertical meridian (UVM) revealed that streamline density is highest along the HM, intermediate along the LVM and lowest along the UVM【854870623446578†L368-L401】. These differences mirror well-known perceptual asymmetries such as the horizontal–vertical (HVA) and vertical meridian (VMA) asymmetries. Meridian-dependent effects were consistently observed across independent cohorts, and control analyses showed that similar asymmetries were absent in long-range connections between visual cortex and the rest of the brain【854870623446578†L418-L431】.

Lifespan analyses revealed that connectivity asymmetries evolve across development and aging. The vertical meridian asymmetry showed a negative quadratic relationship with age, indicating greater sensitivity to developmental and aging processes, whereas the horizontal–vertical asymmetry varied less with age【854870623446578†L433-L449】. Finally, a population-level template was created by aggregating tractography from more than 1,000 participants, enabling recovery of retinotopic connections that are difficult to reconstruct in individual subjects and providing a compact reference for future studies【854870623446578†L499-L534】.

Retinotopic wiring principle

In the preprint, the authors demonstrate that structural connectivity across the human visual cortex preserves retinotopic organization: cortical regions representing the same locations in visual space connect preferentially. They show that well‑known perceptual asymmetries correspond to systematic asymmetries in connectivity within early visual cortex but not in connections linking visual cortex to the rest of the brain【864227417811673†L84-L100】. The study introduces a scalable approach for mapping retinotopic connectivity when tractography alone is unreliable and provides a general principle linking brain structure, function, and behavior.
